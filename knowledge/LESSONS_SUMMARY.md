# Lessons Summary

A condensed, human-readable digest of LESSONS.md — the handful of lessons
that matter most, distilled from the full categorized list. Update this
occasionally; LESSONS.md remains the source of truth.

_Distilled 2026-06-13 from L1–L30._

1. **Build complements to model progress, not applications of it.** Build
   what AI makes scarce — archives, fragmented or adversarially
   hard-to-gather data — never what it makes cheap, and never where every
   model release arms incumbents equally. (L16, L17, L26, L27, L29)

2. **Assume your insight already has a cap table.** Search for competitors
   before building. Categories with named unicorns, or where the real moat
   is capital, are closed to a low-capital founder; early-contested beats
   crowded but loses to empty. (L8, L13, L14, L15)

3. **Respect the founder constraint, not just the market.** Solo + low
   capital disqualifies long sales cycles, enterprise trust walls (SOC 2,
   vendor review), markets with a handful of concentrated buyers, and any
   idea too big for a solo MVP — regardless of TAM. (L3, L5, L6, L20)

4. **Total platform dependency is disqualifying.** If the platform owns
   the interface or registry, it owns you; a huge TAM does not cure it.
   Features of platforms are not companies. (L4, L11)

5. **Demand must be durable and concentrated where errors hurt.**
   Depleting demand pools (one-time recoveries, arbitrage windows) are not
   markets; vertical beats horizontal where mistakes carry legal,
   financial, or safety consequences. (L1, L2)

6. **Optimize the actual bottleneck.** If the constraint is human trust,
   licenses, or signatures, automating the easy 95% of work-hours changes
   nothing — and agent org charts don't fix it either. (L21, L22)

7. **Know which game you're playing.** Founder economics differ from
   investor economics: a capital-light cash-flowing business can be a
   great thing to start and a bad venture bet. The factory compounds;
   individual startups are allowed to fail. (L23, L24, L25)
